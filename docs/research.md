# S&C Programming Agent — Research Foundation

**Purpose:** Knowledge base for an AI agent that writes periodized programs for college-level athletes. This document establishes what the agent must know, where the evidence is strong, where it is contested, and where the field relies on practitioner consensus rather than controlled research.

**Confidence notation used throughout:** HIGH = multiple RCTs or large prospective cohorts with consistent findings. MEDIUM = smaller studies, expert consensus with mechanistic plausibility, or cross-sectional data. LOW = practitioner folklore, single studies, or extrapolation from adjacent populations.

---

## Section 1: The Field's Gold Standards

### 1.1 Certifications and Governing Bodies

**NSCA (National Strength and Conditioning Association)**

The NSCA's CSCS (Certified Strength and Conditioning Specialist) is the de facto minimum qualification for most Division I S&C positions. The exam covers Scientific Foundations (exercise science, sport psychology, nutrition) and Practical/Applied (technique, program design, testing, administration). The NSCA does not prescribe a single periodization model — it treats linear, undulating, and block models as context-dependent tools — but its textbook (*Essentials of Strength Training and Conditioning*, EST&C) and exam lean toward traditional and undulating frameworks, with block periodization receiving increasing coverage in recent editions.

The RSCC (Registered Strength and Conditioning Coach) credential adds an experience and professional practice layer above the CSCS, tiered as RSCC → RSCC*D → RSCC*E by years of full-time collegiate coaching. This attempts to close the gap between exam knowledge and actual coaching competency. NSCA standards are largely implicit — embedded in the EST&C, not a standalone policy document. There is wide interpretive latitude in actual program design.

**CSCCa (Collegiate Strength and Conditioning Coaches Association)**

The SCCC (Strength and Conditioning Coach Certified) is a competing credential used by many college coaches, particularly in football-heavy programs. Key distinction: the SCCC requires documented supervised apprenticeship before examination — apprenticeship-first rather than knowledge-first. The CSCCa membership is almost exclusively collegiate; the credential is viewed by many D-I programs as more relevant to actual collegiate practice than the CSCS. The CSCCa does not publish a comprehensive programming textbook comparable to EST&C; its standards are transmitted through mentorship culture and its annual symposium. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH for institutional characterization]

**UKSCA (United Kingdom Strength and Conditioning Association)**

The UKSCA operates on a competency-framework model with four levels (Foundation → Advanced → Senior → Master). It carries heavier influence from BASES (British Association of Sport and Exercise Sciences) than either NSCA or CSCCa, making it more explicitly tied to peer-reviewed sports science. Distinctive features: formally integrates monitoring and load management (GPS, HRV, wellness) as programming tools rather than optional add-ons; more cautious about maximal 1RM testing with less-trained athletes; reflects the European "performance scientist" model, where sport science and S&C roles overlap more than in American collegiate practice. [CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM-HIGH]

**Other bodies:** ASCA (Australia) mirrors UKSCA structure with heavy influence from AFL and rugby codes, producing strong emphasis on repeated sprint ability, aerobic power development, and concurrent training management. ACSM is primarily health/clinical — its credentials are not recognized equivalents to CSCS or SCCC for collegiate S&C positions. EXOS represents practitioner philosophy rather than credentialing authority; many coaches carry movement quality, corrective exercise, and energy system frameworks absorbed from EXOS-adjacent training.

**Where bodies genuinely disagree (not just semantics):**

- *Olympic lifting priority*: NSCA treats power clean and derivatives as evidence-supported tools. UKSCA and ASCA are more agnostic — some European practitioners argue loaded jumps and trap bar variations produce equivalent transfer with lower technical overhead. This is a real debate (Suchomel et al., 2015, *JSCR* supports Olympic lifting derivatives; practitioner debate continues on implementation burden). [CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]
- *Periodization philosophy*: American mainstream operates within some variant of block or undulating periodization. UK/Australian sport science has been more receptive to Issurin's block model and concurrent load monitoring (GPS/HRV) as primary periodization drivers rather than fixed mesocycle structures.
- *Strength testing frequency*: American D-I programs frequently use periodic 1RM or near-1RM testing. European frameworks more often use velocity-based or submaximal testing to manage fatigue, particularly in-season.

### 1.2 Foundational Textbooks

**Essentials of Strength Training and Conditioning** (NSCA / Haff & Triplett, 4th ed. 2016) — The field's primary codification text. Authority derives from NSCA's adoption as the CSCS exam reference. Competent breadth across physiology, biomechanics, nutrition, testing, and program design. Limitations: reflects the evidence base at time of publication; periodization treatment is more settled than the actual literature supports; distinctly American sport context. It is a professional consensus document, not a current research synthesis. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH for characterization]

**Supertraining** (Siff & Verkhoshansky, 6th ed. 2003) — Load-bearing in how serious practitioners think about special strength development. Genuine contributions: classification of strength types (maximum strength, speed-strength, strength-speed, reactive strength); the conjugate sequence system; treatment of adaptation as non-linear and multi-factorial. Limitation: Soviet-era research is difficult to independently verify; some physiological claims have not held up under contemporary scrutiny; complex enough to invite misapplication. Practitioner-essential but not uncritically accepted. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH for characterization; individual claims: LOW to MEDIUM]

**Science and Practice of Strength Training** (Zatsiorsky & Kraemer, 2nd ed. 2006) — More precisely scientific than *Supertraining*. Unique contribution: mechanistic depth on the three routes to strength gain (hypertrophy, neural drive, inter-muscular coordination), and thorough treatment of dynamic correspondence (specificity of training to sport demands). The most academically defensible of the classical practitioner texts. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

**Periodization: Theory and Methodology of Training** (Bompa & Haff, 5th ed. 2009) — The primary source for traditional linear periodization. Systematized Soviet and Eastern Bloc periodization concepts for Western application. Critics (notably Issurin) argue the traditional macrocycle model is insufficiently concentrated for highly trained athletes and assumes longer residual training effects than evidence supports. This is a substantive disagreement, not semantic. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH for characterization of the debate]

**Block Periodization** (Issurin, 2008, 2016) — Substantive alternative to traditional periodization for advanced athletes. Core argument: highly trained athletes cannot effectively develop multiple physical qualities simultaneously; concentrated blocks targeting 2-3 qualities allow cleaner adaptation before residual effects diminish. Influenced track, swimming, and Olympic sport programming significantly. Team sport applicability remains the practical challenge. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH for theoretical contribution; MEDIUM for team sport applicability]

**Triphasic Training** (Cal Dietz, 2012) — Achieved significant adoption in collegiate football despite limited peer-reviewed evidence base. Core argument: eccentric, isometric, and concentric phases should be trained in concentrated blocks — this draws on real physiology (RFD, tendon loading, reactive strength) but specific sequencing claims are largely supported by practitioner case reports, not controlled trials. Coaches adopted it because it provided a coherent, detailed system with explicit progressions that academic literature rarely delivers. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH for adoption pattern; LOW-MEDIUM for specific protocol evidence]

**Ultimate MMA Conditioning** (Jamieson, 2009) — Relevant in combat sports; has cross-pollinated into team sport conditioning through its energy system development framework and HRV-based recovery monitoring. [CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM for applied claims]

### 1.3 The Research Frontier (2024–2025)

**Velocity-Based Training (VBT)**

*Settled:* The load-velocity relationship in major compound lifts is well-established and highly individualized within an individual across time (Gonzalez-Badillo & Sanchez-Medina, 2010, *JSCR*; Jovanovic & Flanagan, 2014, *IJSPP*). Minimum velocity thresholds are reasonably stable across training status.

*Debated:* Whether VBT-guided autoregulation meaningfully outperforms well-implemented RPE in trained athletes is genuinely unsettled. Device reliability is a real concern — linear position transducers (GymAware) show strong validity; accelerometer-based devices (Push Band) have greater measurement error particularly at higher loads (Weakley et al., 2021, *JSCR*). VBT feedback's motivational effect on acute velocity has decent support; long-term superiority over simpler methods is less established. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH for methodology; MEDIUM for superiority claims]

**RPE and Autoregulation**

Session RPE (Foster et al., 2001, *JSCR*) and repetitions-in-reserve (Zourdos et al., 2016, *JSCR*) have solid evidence bases for trained individuals. Accuracy degrades under psychological fatigue, competitive stress, and in early training careers. Group training sessions and time constraints make precise autoregulation harder than laboratory conditions imply. The evidence is real; the college implementation is consistently messier. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH for evidence base; MEDIUM for team sport feasibility]

**Conjugate vs. Block vs. DUP**

"Conjugate" as used in collegiate practice frequently bears little resemblance to Westside's original formulation. Most coaches using the term mean concurrent multi-quality development within a training week. The DUP research (Rhea et al., 2002, *JSCR*; Miranda et al., 2011, *JSCR*) shows comparable or superior strength gains to LP in intermediate-to-advanced trainees, but many studies have ecological validity limitations for elite collegiate athletes. Block periodization evidence in team sport is sparse and methodologically limited. [CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM for comparative research]

**Concurrent Training Interference**

The interference effect is real but frequently overstated (Wilson et al., 2012, *JSCR*, meta-analysis). Interference primarily affects hypertrophy, less so maximal strength, and minimally power compared to endurance. Practical guidance: separate strength and endurance by ≥6-8 hours when possible; prioritize strength before endurance in a concurrent session; cycling interferes more with lower body strength adaptation than running in non-cyclists. In team sport athletes where repeated sprint ability is the primary endurance quality, interference concern is substantially reduced. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH for meta-analytic findings; MEDIUM for precise thresholds]

**Force-Velocity Profiling**

Morin & Samozino's work (2016, *IJSPP*; Samozino et al., 2012, *Med Sci Sports Exerc*) on individualized F-V profiling is mechanistically sound. Athletes can be "force-deficient" or "velocity-deficient" relative to their theoretical optimal F-V profile. Whether individualized training based on F-V profiles produces better outcomes than volume-equated non-individualized programming is inadequately tested. Theory is strong; intervention evidence is preliminary. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH for theoretical model; LOW-MEDIUM for outcome superiority]

**Post-Activation Potentiation/Performance (PAP)**

Complex training has genuine physiological rationale (myosin light chain phosphorylation, neural drive). Meta-analyses support PAP effects (Seitz & Haff, 2016, *Sports Med*), but individual variability is substantial and training status is a major moderator. The optimal rest interval (8-12 minutes for maximal PAP in trained athletes) is typically incompatible with team training logistics. Evidence for shorter intervals producing meaningful PAP in group settings is weaker. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH for PAP existence; MEDIUM for optimal implementation; LOW for typical team protocols]

**Sleep and Recovery**

Sleep extension improves performance in college athletes (Mah et al., 2011, *Sleep*). Chronic sleep restriction impairs recovery, force production, hormonal function, and skill acquisition — consistently documented. Cold water immersion attenuates muscle protein synthesis acutely (Fyfe et al., 2019, *J Physiol*) — important to know when used post-strength training. HRV-guided periodization has plausible rationale but limited high-quality team sport trials. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH for sleep; MEDIUM-LOW for most recovery modalities]

**Critical cross-cutting flag:** Most S&C research uses recreationally trained college students as subjects, not NCAA Division I athletes with 2-4 years of serious training. Ecological validity is a systemic problem. Extrapolation from research populations to elite collegiate athletes should be flagged consistently by the agent.

## Section 2: Sport-Specific Demands

### 2.1 Field / Invasion Sports (Football, Soccer, Lacrosse, Field Hockey, Rugby)

**Bioenergetic demands:**
- *American football:* ATP-PC dominant (~80-90% of in-play demand); work-to-rest ratio 1:6–1:8 for skill positions. Oxidative capacity matters for between-series recovery, not play execution — a distinction coaches understand intellectually but frequently underprogram for. [CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]
- *Soccer:* Outfield players cover 10-13 km/match; ~40-60 sprints per match (2-4 seconds each). Energy system: ~70% oxidative, 20% glycolytic, 10% ATP-PC. The oxidative fraction is commonly overweighted in programming; repeated-sprint ability (RSA) under incomplete recovery is the actual performance limiter for most positions. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH — Bangsbo, Mohr, Krustrup research is extensive]
- *Lacrosse:* Between football and soccer in sprint density and duration. *Rugby:* Union (chaotic, extended phases) vs. league (structured, 6-tackle sets) differ substantially — both demand significant collision tolerance and upper-body structural integrity that cardio-centric programs routinely underaddress.

**Injury epidemiology:**
- ACL rates in women's soccer and basketball exceed male counterparts by 2-8x depending on study (NCAA ISP data). Female collegiate soccer: ~0.33 per 1,000 athlete-exposures. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH]
- Hamstring injuries in male soccer: recurrence rate 12-34% within first month post-RTP. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH]
- FIFA 11+: 30-50% overall injury reduction, 50% hamstring injury reduction when compliance is high. The compliance caveat is critical — poor adherence produces minimal effect, and college-level compliance is inconsistent. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH for protocol; MEDIUM for real-world effectiveness]
- Nordic hamstring curls: ~51% reduction in hamstring injury rates with consistent use (van Dyk et al., 2019, systematic review). Chronically underutilized because athletes find them aversive.
- Ankle sprains: most common injury across all field sports. Proprioceptive training reduces recurrence by 35-50% in athletes with prior history; primary prevention is less supported. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH for secondary prevention]

**Position-specific needs:**
- *Linemen:* Maximal force production, collision absorption, 60-80 repeated short efforts per game. Do not need significant aerobic base beyond baseline. Programming them like skill positions — excessive conditioning, single-leg dominant work, minimal upper-body pulling — is the most common error in collegiate football S&C.
- *Skill positions (WR, DB, RB):* Maximal velocity development, COD, aerobic capacity to maintain sprint quality through four quarters.
- *Soccer GK:* Explosive short-distance acceleration (2-5m), upper-body power, landing mechanics. Low aerobic demand relative to field players. Running GKs through outfield conditioning protocols is an arbitrary tradition with no sport-specific justification.

**In-season structure:**
- Football's weekly structure (Saturday game) is the most studied microcycle. Day-after-game recovery → mid-week peak → pre-game taper. Classic 3-day structure (Monday activation, Wednesday heavy, Thursday neural prime) is well-established. Error: applying this as a universal template.
- Soccer with mid-week fixtures compresses to 72-hour blocks — cannot support the same strength volume. Research (Mujika, Impellizzeri) consistently shows 1x/week heavy compound lifting at reduced volume can maintain strength in-season. This remains underimplemented due to sport coach resistance.

**Common programming mistakes:**
- Excessive unilateral volume for athletes who primarily generate force bilaterally (linemen, rugby props)
- Skipping heavy posterior chain work for soccer players (Nordic curls, RDLs, trap bar deadlifts)
- Neglecting deceleration training — the most underaddressed component across all field sports. Eccentric quad and hip strength in high-velocity deceleration is the biomechanical antecedent to ACL injury

### 2.2 Court Sports (Basketball, Volleyball, Tennis)

**Bioenergetic demands:**
- *Basketball:* ~105 high-intensity efforts/game; 4-5 km total distance; ~1,000 COD. Energy: ~55% oxidative, 35% glycolytic, 10% ATP-PC. Seasons run 30-40 games over 5 months. [CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]
- *Volleyball:* Middle blockers attack and block at maximal intensity on nearly every touch. Rally duration 3-8 seconds; 10-20 seconds between points — ATP-PC dominant for attackers, higher oxidative for liberos.
- *Tennis:* Point duration 4-10 seconds, rest 20-25 seconds — genuinely ATP-PC dominant for most points with glycolytic contribution in extended points. Widely mischaracterized as primarily aerobic because matches are long.

**Injury epidemiology:**
- Patellar tendinopathy ("jumper's knee") prevalence in elite volleyball: ~45% (Lian et al.). A load-management problem, not fixable with isolation exercises. Primary intervention once symptomatic: progressive heavy slow resistance (HSR). [CONFIDENCE: HIGH for HSR protocol]
- ACL rates in female basketball comparable to female soccer. Ankle sprains highest of court sports, rival field sports.
- Volleyball shoulder injuries ~30% of all volleyball injuries. Serving motion (IR, elevation) with 200-300 shoulder actions per practice session creates cumulative stress requiring dedicated shoulder health programming.
- Stress fractures in volleyball: lumbar spine (pars), tibias, metatarsals — elevated in female athletes, athletes with RED-S risk, and year-round club players.

**Position-specific needs:**
- *Volleyball middles:* Attack and block at near-maximal intensity every touch. Fast SSC work (contact times <200ms) is the primary physical quality gap. Depth jumps and box jumps are done competently by most programs; the specific fast SSC stimulus that transfers to blocking is often absent.
- *Basketball PG vs. center:* PGs perform more lateral COD; centers perform more short-sprint and collision work. Programming them identically ignores meaningful demand differences.

**In-season:**
- Basketball's 30-40 game season makes strength improvement unrealistic for most of the season. Maintaining with 1-2x/week sessions is the realistic target. Early morning lift traditions exist for scheduling convenience, not physiological rationale.
- Double-practice preseason cultures: introducing high-volume, high-intensity strength during two-a-days is counterproductive. Emphasize quality over volume with recovery prioritized.

**Common mistakes:**
- Excessive eccentric quad loading mid-season for volleyball players (patellar tendinopathy risk in a population already at or near tendon stress ceiling from jump volume)
- Adding jump volume in-season without accounting for practice jump count
- Ignoring shoulder health work for volleyball players

### 2.3 Combat Sports (Wrestling, Judo, Boxing)

**Bioenergetic demands:**
- *Wrestling (NCAA):* Three 2-minute periods, 30-second rest intervals. Work-to-rest ~4:1 during periods. Energy: ~45% aerobic, ~40% glycolytic, ~15% ATP-PC. Aerobic component is higher than most coaches estimate; VO2max is a meaningful performance predictor. [CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]
- *Boxing:* 3-minute rounds, 1-minute rest. High-exchange bouts are highly glycolytic; strategic bouts are more oxidative. Glycolytic buffering capacity is the primary limiter in competitive boxing.
- *Judo:* 2-3 minutes of continuous effort; explosive grip strength and repeated high-intensity throwing make this one of the most demanding combined strength-endurance sports.

**Weight class implications:**
- Rapid weight loss (2-5% body mass in 24-48 hours) impairs strength, power, cognitive function, and anaerobic capacity. Evidence is unambiguous (multiple controlled studies: Ransone, Barbas, et al.). Programming during active cut phases must account for reduced capacity. Heavy training during active cuts is contraindicated — neural recruitment is impaired, injury risk increases. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH]
- The S&C coach's role is not to facilitate cutting but to help athletes achieve competition weight without acute dehydration.

**Injury patterns:** Shoulder (AC joint, rotator cuff, labral tears), knee (MCL, meniscus) dominant in wrestling. Skin infections (ringworm, MRSA) are a unique facility hygiene concern.

**Common mistakes:**
- Excessive cardiovascular conditioning when sport practice already provides high aerobic demand — add fatigue without addressing limiting energy systems
- Ignoring grip strength and isometric training, which are among the clearest cases of genuinely sport-specific training with direct transfer

### 2.4 Endurance Sports (Track Distance, Cross Country, Swimming, Rowing)

**Concurrent training — key findings:**
- Interference effect: real but overstated. Wilson et al. (2012) meta-analysis: concurrent training reduces hypertrophy by ~31% and power gains by ~28% vs. strength-only; absolute strength gains largely preserved. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH]
- Strength training for endurance athletes: heavy compound lower-body work 2x/week improves running economy 2-8% in trained runners (Taipale, Beattie, Ronnestad). Mechanism is primarily neural efficiency, not hypertrophy. Target: maximize neural adaptation with minimal hypertrophy — low volume, heavy load, high velocity. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH]
- Bilateral squatting for runners is sometimes discouraged in favor of purely unilateral work — this is a case where "sport-specific" marketing overrides biology. Bilateral squatting develops maximal force production that expresses through better running mechanics and fatigue resistance.

**In-season programming:** Two sessions/week, 30-45 minutes each, scheduled on days without long or high-intensity endurance sessions. Minimal volume (2-4 sets/exercise, 3-5 exercises), high intensity. The common mistake: 5x10 moderate-load accessory work adds fatigue without the neural stimulus that transfers to performance.

**Injury epidemiology:**
- Stress fractures in female endurance athletes: most consequential injury class, strongly associated with LOW energy availability (RED-S). S&C cannot compensate without dietitian coordination. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH]
- ITB syndrome: widely attributed to hip weakness, but evidence for hip strengthening as primary treatment is weaker than commonly presented. Load management is the primary driver. [CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM — mechanistically contested]

---

### 2.5 Power / Track and Field (Sprints, Jumps, Throws)

- All sprint and jump events are ATP-PC dominant. 100m: ~98% anaerobic. 400m: ~55% anaerobic (glycolytic), 45% oxidative — most metabolically demanding event relative to duration. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH]
- Periodization: the clearest example of classical LP logic in college sport. Defined indoor/outdoor calendar maps directly to accumulation → strength → power sequence. Mistake: treating every block identically across both seasons without managing cumulative fatigue.
- Heavy overhead pressing for javelin athletes can reinforce external rotation positions that impair acceleration mechanics. Power clean improvements transfer to shot put through improved hip extension RFD. Technical skill interaction requires close S&C/event coach coordination — a relationship that is often antagonistic in practice. [CONFIDENCE: LOW-MEDIUM for mechanic transfer claims]
- **Biggest mistake:** Excessive hypertrophy programming for sprinters. The world's fastest athletes train for RFD and maximal velocity, not muscle size. Adding unnecessary body mass impairs power-to-weight ratio. Low-load, high-velocity work should dominate competition preparation for sprint/jump athletes.

---

### 2.6 Aesthetic / Judged Sports (Gymnastics, Diving, Skating, Cheerleading)

- RED-S risk: highest of any sport group. Gymnastics, diving, and figure skating have documented disordered eating prevalence of 15-40% depending on measurement method. The S&C coach must never comment on body composition targets or suggest weight manipulation. Performance goals (jump height, strength ratios) are the appropriate framing.
- Gymnasts exhibit relative strength ratios (force:bodyweight) exceeding most other athletic populations. Training volume (20-30 hours/week) leaves genuinely minimal recovery bandwidth for additional strength work. S&C role is maintenance and injury prevention, not performance gains beyond what skill training provides.
- Programming constraint: sport coach often runs conditioning; S&C coaches may have limited time or authority. Establishing trust through clearly non-aesthetic, performance-focused goals is critical for adoption.

---

### 2.7 Overhead / Rotational Sports (Baseball, Softball, Golf, Swimming — rotational emphasis)

- **UCL loading:** Collegiate pitchers throw 100-200+ pitches/week across practice and competition. UCL reconstruction rates have increased ~5x in the past 20 years in pitching athletes (Andrews, Lyman, and others — epidemiological data is robust). S&C contribution: thoracic mobility maintenance (reduces compensatory elbow/shoulder stress), posterior shoulder flexibility, scapular stability — not arm strengthening.
- **Heavy overhead pressing for pitchers during competitive season is contraindicated** based on biomechanical and clinical evidence. Rotator cuff is already operating at high eccentric loading tolerances during deceleration; adding overhead press volume increases structural risk without proportionate benefit. [CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM — clinical consensus strong; controlled trials difficult]
- Bench press for pitchers is legitimately debated. Some programs avoid in-season due to pectoralis shortening concerns; others maintain with careful volume management. The agent should surface this debate rather than resolve it confidently.
- **Anti-rotation core work** (Pallof press, landmine, cable chop with controlled deceleration) directly trains limiting tissue for oblique strains in batters. [CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

---

### 2.8 Cross-Cutting Analysis

**Where sports genuinely differ:**
- Collision tolerance requirements (football linemen vs. everyone else)
- Cumulative throwing volume and specific injury implications (baseball/softball pitchers vs. everyone else)
- Aesthetic/judged sports' RED-S risk profile

**Where coaches overestimate differences:**
The posterior chain, deceleration mechanics, hip stability, and basic power development requirements are fundamentally similar across soccer, basketball, volleyball, lacrosse, and track athletes. The same foundational template — trap bar deadlift, single-leg RDL, lateral bound, sprint mechanics — could serve as the base for all with position/sport modifications in volume, intensity, and accessory selection. The market for "sport-specific" programming packages exploits perceived differences that often do not substantially change programming logic.

**Sports where "sport-specific" is mostly marketing:** Golf S&C, volleyball-specific plyometrics, "baseball strength" — these apply basic principles with sport-colored packaging. The fundamentals are universal.

**Sports where genuine specialization is necessary:** Pitching athletes (UCL mechanics, shoulder health), gymnasts (relative strength demands, RED-S), weight-cutting combat athletes. Error modes here are both specific and consequential.

## Section 3: Periodization Models

### 3.1 Linear Periodization (LP)

**Origins:** Matveyev's empirically observed training wave — high-volume/low-intensity preparatory → low-volume/high-intensity competition. Bompa systematized for Western application. The progressive overload mechanism is mechanistically sound and has strong evidence for novice-to-intermediate populations (Peterson et al., 2011, *JSCR* meta-analysis). [CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

**When it works:** Beginners and early-intermediate athletes; single-peak sports with defined preparation-to-competition sequences (track & field, powerlifting, Olympic lifting). Predictability is a genuine advantage — planning is precise, compliance is easier to monitor.

**When it fails:** Multi-peak team sport environments; extended detraining of sport-specific qualities during accumulation phases; long cycles accumulating fatigue in ways that are difficult to manage without experienced oversight. The "supercompensation" timing assumption was largely theoretical, not derived from rigorous controlled trials.

**Critical distinction:** What most coaches call "linear periodization" — simply adding weight each week on the same exercises across a semester — is not periodization in any meaningful sense. It lacks phase transitions and wave structure. True LP involves deliberate phase shifts in emphasis. Conflating linear loading with linear periodization is one of the most persistent misunderstandings in practical S&C.

---

### 3.2 Block Periodization (BP)

**Issurin's model vs. Bompa's complex training:** Issurin (2008, 2016) concentrates entire blocks (2-6 weeks) on 2-3 targeted qualities while deliberately suppressing others, rather than developing multiple qualities simultaneously throughout a phase. The interference-avoidance logic has theoretical support; team sport application is the practical challenge. [CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

**Residual training effects:** Issurin's reported residuals (aerobic endurance: 25-35 days; maximal strength: 30 days; glycolytic capacity: 18-24 days; explosive strength: 15 days) provide sequencing rationale — train longest-residual qualities first, shortest last, so all peak simultaneously. These figures come primarily from elite Eastern European athletes and have not been replicated with comparable rigor in Western populations. Treat them as directional heuristics, not precise values.

**Practical block sequence** for college strength-power athlete: Accumulation (4 weeks, high volume, moderate intensity, hypertrophy/aerobic base) → Transmutation (3-4 weeks, moderate volume, high intensity, maximal strength and strength-speed) → Realization (1-2 weeks, low volume, high intensity, power expression and peaking).

**When it fails:** Athletes who cannot sustain concentrated loading volume; insufficient training age; short preparation windows (<6 weeks before competition).

---

### 3.3 Conjugate Periodization

**What it actually is:** Simmons's model rotates maximal effort (ME) and dynamic effort (DE) days with frequent exercise variation to prevent accommodation. The name comes from the Soviet concept of conjugate sequencing (developing multiple qualities in close temporal proximity). The legitimate scientific basis rests on Zatsiorsky's maximal effort method and velocity-specificity of power training. College adaptations typically modify exercise rotation frequency to suit lower training ages.

**When conjugate works:** Advanced powerlifters, athletes needing concurrent quality development across long competitive calendars with no single peak. Genuine merit for concurrent multi-quality development.

**When it fails:** Technical sport athletes (frequent variation disrupts motor pattern consolidation); beginners (cannot take advantage of the system while developing movement competency); in-season (variation creates inconsistent fatigue patterns).

**Key criticism:** Exercise rotation masks fitness gains and makes progress tracking harder. Distinguishing true adaptation from movement learning on new exercise variants requires careful record-keeping across exercise families. [CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM — practitioner evidence strong; controlled research for team sport athletes limited]

---

### 3.4 Daily Undulating Periodization (DUP)

**Evidence base:** Rhea et al. (2002, *JSCR*) found DUP produced significantly greater strength gains than LP over 12 weeks in trained men. This is frequently cited but requires qualification: small sample, LP protocol may not represent optimal implementation, effect sizes should not be overgeneralized. Miranda et al. (2011) and subsequent research produced mixed results. Systematic view: DUP is at least equivalent to LP for intermediate-to-advanced athletes and may offer advantages in longer blocks where LP monotony leads to accommodation. [CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM-HIGH — multiple studies with methodological variability]

**Practical implementation** (in-season football example): Monday (strength, 85-95% 1RM, 3-5 reps) → Wednesday (power, 60-75% 1RM, explosive intent) → Friday (hypertrophy, 65-75% 1RM, 8-12 reps). Maintains strength, power expression, and mass simultaneously — significant advantage during long competitive seasons.

**Limitations:** Requires more sophisticated programming than LP; interaction between stimuli within compressed weekly timeframe increases fatigue accumulation risk if volume is poorly managed; unnecessary for true beginners who adapt to any reasonable stimulus.

---

### 3.5 Agile / Reactive Periodization

**Framework:** Training modified based on actual athlete state rather than predetermined templates, integrating HRV monitoring, wellness questionnaires (Hooper Index), and readiness scales.

**Honest evidence assessment:** HRV-guided training superiority over well-planned traditional periodization is not established. Kiviniemi et al. (2007, *EJAP*) showed HRV-guided training improved aerobic performance more than traditional in recreational runners — this has not been convincingly replicated in strength training with team sport athletes. The monitoring infrastructure required is operationally burdensome for many college programs. [CONFIDENCE: LOW-MEDIUM — monitoring value is real; outcome superiority over good planning is not established]

**Best practical use:** Supplement, not replace, planned periodization. Use monitoring to modify sessions at the margins — adjust volume, delay a max effort test, convert high-intensity day to technical work when readiness signals are poor. The daily go/no-go call, not the plan.

---

### 3.6 In-Season Programming

**The operational reality:** This is where periodization theory most frequently collides with practice. Basketball (30+ games over 4 months), football (1 game/week), volleyball (bi-weekly) all produce different session availability. The fundamental programming question shifts from "how do we build fitness" to "how do we maintain fitness at minimum cost."

**Can you improve strength in-season?** Yes, conditionally. Kraemer et al. (2004, *JSCR*) demonstrated strength maintenance and modest gains are achievable with ≥2 sessions/week at adequate intensity in college football players. Volume can be substantially reduced (30-50%); intensity must be preserved at ≥80% 1RM. Improvement is more likely early in season when recovery is adequate. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH for maintenance principle]

**Practical microcycle framework** (Monday/Wednesday/Friday, Saturday competition): Monday (moderate volume/moderate-to-high intensity, strength emphasis, 48+ hours post-game) → Wednesday (lower volume, higher intensity or contrast work, power emphasis) → Friday (minimal volume, low fatigue, neural activation/priming).

**Travel and academic load:** Travel programming is typically ad hoc when it should be planned. A road trip eliminating a Wednesday session requires proactive load compensation — either front-loading or restructuring post-travel session. Academic stress (midterms, finals) produces measurable cortisol elevations and genuinely affects recovery. Scheduling deloads to coincide with high-academic-demand weeks is legitimate periodization.

**Position-specific in-season adjustments:** Starters vs. reserves require different volume management. Linemen accumulate more physical contact load per game than skill positions — require more conservative volume management in-season.

**Minimum effective dose:** Bickel et al. (2011, *Med Sci Sports Exerc*) — 1 session/week at moderate-to-high intensity can maintain strength for 4-8 weeks in trained athletes. Conservative in-season target: 2 sessions/week at ≥70-80% 1RM. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

---

### 3.7 Deload Logic

**Evidence for timing:** Specific deload frequency prescriptions (every 3rd, 4th, or 6th week) come primarily from practitioner tradition, not direct experimental comparison of frequencies. What research does support: fatigue accumulation masks fitness, strategic volume reduction produces supercompensation-like responses, chronic training without planned recovery increases injury risk. [CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM for principle; LOW for specific timing]

**Default recommendation:** Every 4 weeks for most college athletes in structured programs. Active deloads (reduced volume/intensity but continued training) are generally superior to passive deloads for maintaining movement quality and psychological routine. Passive deloads appropriate post-season, post-illness, or during major life stressors.

**Team sport complexity:** Games are a physiological stimulus — high-game-density weeks effectively function as de facto deloads; formal training volume should be adjusted accordingly. Don't impose a deload on top of game stress.

**Recommended approach:** Scheduled deloads as default (more reliable than waiting for coaches/athletes to accurately identify accumulating fatigue, which is systematically underestimated), with permission to insert additional deloads based on objective monitoring data.

---

### 3.8 Return-to-Play Programming

**Who makes the call:** Return-to-sport decisions are appropriately made by sports medicine physicians and licensed athletic trainers, not S&C coaches. The S&C role begins with progressive loading design once medical clearance for exercise has been granted. This boundary has legal and safety implications that cannot be blurred.

**Criteria-based vs. time-based:** Criteria-based approaches are increasingly supported. Kyritsis et al. (2016, *BJSM*) found that ACL return-to-sport without completing functional criteria was associated with 4-6x greater reinjury risk. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH for major lower extremity injuries]

**RTP programming progression:**
1. Non-weight-bearing: upper body and contralateral limb maintenance; isometric work at available ROM as cleared; non-loading cardiovascular
2. Partial weight-bearing: bilateral movements with load shifted to tolerance; neuromuscular control introduction
3. Full weight-bearing, non-sport-specific: bilateral to progressive unilateral; force production assessment; power progression
4. Sport-specific loading: COD, deceleration, reactive tasks, position-specific demands
5. Full-contact practice integration

**Most consequential RTP error:** Loading athletes to 1RM before neuromuscular reactive control is rebuilt. Muscle cross-sectional area recovers before neuromuscular coordination — creating a deceptive window where athletes are strong on bilateral assessments but lack sport-required reactive stabilization. Rebuilding force production before reactive control is established is the second-most-common RTP error after premature return timing. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

## Section 4: The Individualization Layer

### 4.1 Training Age

Training age is not a calendar metric. A 4-year college athlete who played varsity high school sports may have thousands of hours of practice with no systematic progressive overload, no programmed movement quality work, and no planned periodization. That athlete is not an advanced trainee by any meaningful programming standard.

Assess as a composite of: movement competency, exposure to systematic progressive overload, exercise selection history, breadth of energy system development.

**The adaptation hierarchy:** Structural before neural before metabolic. Connective tissue (tendons, ligaments, cartilage) adapts 6-12 weeks slower than muscle contractile tissue (Magnusson et al., 2010, *J Physiol*). Early strength gains are primarily from motor learning and intramuscular coordination. Programming for low-training-age athletes should emphasize movement quality, consistency, and moderate volume over novelty or advanced intensification.

**Common error:** Promoting a second-year athlete to advanced programming because they are physically gifted. Athletic ability is not a proxy for training age.

**Quick assessment:** Watch goblet squat, RDL, and push-up. Ask heaviest squat/pull/press relative to bodyweight. Probe programming history — written program vs. "just lifted." Five minutes provides most of what you need to classify novice/intermediate/advanced.

---

### 4.2 Injury History

Not all injury history is programming-relevant. The distinction: information that changes exercise selection vs. information that changes load/volume management vs. information that is recorded and ignored.

**What actually changes programming:** Chronic or recurrent injuries (particularly for structures with limited vascular supply — patellar tendinopathy, Achilles tendinopathy, labral pathology); post-surgical movement restrictions; documented structural pathology.

**What is often falsely treated as a contraindication:** "Can't squat because of knee pain" is usually a coaching problem, not a contraindication. Hartmann et al. (2013, *Sports Med*) found no evidence that deep squatting with appropriate technique is harmful to healthy knees. Anterior knee pain from poor hip control, excessive anterior pelvic tilt, or rapid load progression requires technical correction and load reduction — not exercise substitution. Removing the squat removes the stimulus to fix the problem.

**S&C jurisdiction:** Can independently modify load, volume, tempo, and range of motion; can substitute variations within a movement pattern (box squat for free squat). Cannot return athletes from injury, modify post-surgical protocols, or override physician restrictions. If the modification is programming-based, S&C owns it. If it touches pathology management, it requires clearance.

---

### 4.3 Position and Sport-Specific Role

Position differences are real but routinely over-indexed by coaches seeking to justify expensive sport-specific programming.

**Legitimate position differentiation factors:**
- Force-velocity profile requirements (lineman: maximal force; receiver: RFD and velocity)
- Injury risk profile (lineman: lower extremity overuse, shoulder; QB: shoulder demands)
- Positional energy system demands

**Error to avoid:** Letting differentiation consume early training career. Both a lineman and a receiver need a broad general physical preparation base. Specificity earns value in the last 20% of physical preparation, not the foundation.

---

### 4.4 Testing Data — Signal vs. Noise

**Force plate — CMJ:** Most information-dense test relative to burden. Provides peak power, jump height, braking/propulsive impulse, limb asymmetry. RSImod (jump height / time to takeoff) is a validated neuromuscular readiness and leg stiffness indicator (McMahon et al., 2018, *J Appl Biomech*). A decrease >5-7% from individual baseline indicates accumulated fatigue. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH for readiness monitoring; MEDIUM for injury risk via asymmetry — Bishop et al. (2019, *IJSPP*) found asymmetry thresholds have poor sensitivity/specificity for injury prediction as standalone metrics]

**IMTP:** Measures peak isometric force and impulse. Primary use: benchmarking strength relative to bodyweight. Below 1.5x BW (female) or 1.8x BW (male) suggests maximal strength development should remain the priority over power or speed training. Most reliable force plate protocol for between-session comparison. Limitation: poor ecological validity for dynamic sport demands.

**Drop jump RSI:** Informs plyometric programming tier. RSI below 1.0 suggests insufficient readiness for plyometric intensification — build strength base first. CMJ-to-drop-jump RSI ratio indicates whether athlete is limited by RFD or maximal force capacity. [CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM — thresholds based on practitioner consensus and normative data, not controlled intervention trials]

**VBT load-velocity profiling:** Identifies force-deficient vs. velocity-deficient athletes on the F-V regression line. Velocity loss % guides individualized volume prescription (Pareja-Blanco et al., 2017, *Eur J Sport Sci*): 20% velocity loss → strength; 40% → hypertrophy. Real barriers: equipment cost ($300-$1,500/unit), learning curve, team setting compliance. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH for methodology; MEDIUM for team implementation]

**Sprint testing:** 10m split is the single most important metric for programming decisions — represents trainable acceleration capacity and horizontal force application. Sprint F-V profiling (Morin & Samozino, 2016) identifies horizontally vs. vertically force dominant athletes — guides sled training vs. plyometric emphasis respectively. Broad jump norms exist across positions and sports (Loturco et al., *JSCR*). [CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM-HIGH]

**FMS — the honest assessment:** Moran et al. (2017, *Sports Med*) systematic review of 24 studies found composite FMS score has low sensitivity and specificity for injury prediction across athletic populations. Cook et al.'s original claims have not replicated consistently. Use FMS for identifying gross movement limitations and asymmetries to inform exercise selection — not to stratify injury risk or make return-to-training decisions. [CONFIDENCE: LOW for injury prediction; HIGH for rejecting FMS as a standalone risk tool]

---

### 4.5 Recovery and Schedule Constraints

**Sleep:** Below 6 hours chronically — impairs force production, cognitive function, hormonal recovery (Simpson et al., 2017, *Sleep Med Rev*). College athletes are chronically underslept. Programming implication: athletes with chronic sleep restriction should be in volume-managed programs, not maximal loading phases, regardless of what the periodization schedule says. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

**Academic load:** Exam periods produce sleep disruption, caloric dysregulation, and attentional load that degrades training quality. Appropriate response: reduce volume 30-40% during exam weeks while maintaining intensity. Not reducing intensity — reducing volume.

**HRV:** Consumer apps (HRV4Training, Oura, Whoop) have reasonable group-level accuracy with high individual variability. Alcohol, inconsistent measurement timing, and poor individual compliance degrade data quality significantly. Appropriate use: flag persistent downward trends over 5-7 days, not day-to-day modification. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH for endurance athletes; MEDIUM-LOW for strength-dominant populations]

---

### 4.6 Signal vs. Noise — The Expert Summary

**If you can collect only 5 data points per athlete, collect:**
1. Training age (assessed through movement and history)
2. Injury history (only chronic and structural)
3. Recent sleep and wellness (5-item survey)
4. Sprint split time (10m)
5. Baseline jump test (CMJ height or broad jump)

**Tests that consume resources without proportionally improving programming decisions:**
- FMS as injury prediction tool
- Single-session HRV readings
- One-time flexibility assessments without functional context
- Sport-specific power tests without longitudinal tracking

**The harder judgment:** Population-level evidence often overrides individual data for athletes with low training age. The best-available general programming model for a novice trainee is almost always more appropriate than a heavily individualized protocol based on limited data. Spend individualization resources on athletes whose response profiles have diverged from the population mean — typically those with 3+ years of serious training.

**Where individual data reliably changes programming:**
- Chronic injury structure
- Limb asymmetry >15% on CMJ (address bilaterally)
- Force-velocity profile (once VBT is established)
- Persistent wellness suppression over 5+ days
- Sprint split deficiency relative to position norms

**The honest statement on individualization:** The foundational program elements — progressive overload, compound movement emphasis, sprint and jump development, positional strength standards — are common across nearly all college athletes. The individualization layer refines at the margins, catches the athlete who cannot tolerate a movement, and adjusts load/volume in response to recovery status. It does not replace the program. The coaches producing the best athletes almost always have the most disciplined standard model, not the most individualized one.

## Section 5: The Technology Landscape — Where AI Has Been Tried

### 5.1 Current Platforms — Honest Assessment

**TeamBuildr** — Dominant at NCAA D-I and D-II. Core function: workout builder and athlete-facing delivery system. Coaches construct programs manually; athletes log results via app. Its "AI" feature is pattern-matching against a pre-built template library — not generative reasoning. No adaptive programming based on performance data. Strong for managing large multi-sport rosters, handling scheduling, and athletic director compliance visibility. Meaningful limitations: no native monitoring hardware integration, limited force-plate data ingestion, no capacity to reason about individual athlete context. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

**Bridge Athletic** — Built around sport-specific template libraries developed with sport scientists. Coaches select from validated templates and modify. Periodization embedded in templates rather than dynamically generated. Strong template quality and movement pattern taxonomy. Limitations: significant customization labor for unusual athlete profiles; no reasoning or feedback about programming choices surfaced; no adaptive layer between performance data and future programming. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

**Volt Athletics** — Makes aggressive "AI" marketing claims. Actual mechanism: algorithm-based template progression adjusting volume/intensity based on logged performance against pre-set rules (e.g., if athlete achieves all reps, advance to next load tier). This is progressive overload automation, not machine learning or reasoning. Designed initially for high schools without dedicated S&C staff — fills a genuine gap there. At the college level, removes coach control and operates as a black box. No independent peer-reviewed validation. [CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM]

**TrainHeroic** — Targets powerlifting and strength sports more than team sport college programs. Marketplace model where coaches publish programs; athletes follow. Better coach-athlete communication layer than most competitors. Team management features less mature than TeamBuildr or Bridge. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

**CoachMePlus (CMP)** — Athlete monitoring and readiness platform, not a programming tool. Collects wellness surveys and performance data; alerts coaches when readiness scores fall below thresholds. Value is data centralization and trend visualization. Coaches must interpret CMP data and manually adjust programming — the system does not close that loop. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

**Catapult / STATSports / Kinexon** — GPS/IMU platforms. Dominant load monitoring in team sports. Produce total distance, high-speed running distance, accelerations/decelerations, PlayerLoad. These are measurement tools, not decision tools. The gap between "athlete ran 8,400m with 47 high-speed efforts" and "reduce lower body strength volume 20% tomorrow" is entirely bridged by the coach. Equipment pricing is substantial ($40,000-$100,000+ annually for large programs). [CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

**VALD Performance (ForceDecks, NordBord)** — Testing ecosystem: force plates, hamstring testing, isometric dynamometry. VALD Hub provides visualization and longitudinal tracking. Does not translate test results into programming prescriptions — presents data, leaves decision to coach. Primary application: RTP benchmarking and monitoring. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

**ChatGPT / Claude used directly** — This is already happening. Coaches are using general-purpose LLMs to generate program templates, write exercise progressions, and answer sport science questions — anecdotally widespread and growing despite limited public discussion. Quality for well-specified conventional programming tasks is genuinely reasonable. Known failure modes: hallucinated exercise names, fabricated citations, overconfident RTP outputs, significant quality degradation with complex constraint sets. No persistent memory, no athlete data integration, no mechanism to verify currency of recommendations. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH]

**Injury prediction tools (Kitman Labs, Smartabase)** — ML on monitoring datasets to flag elevated injury risk. Van Eetvelde et al. (2021 systematic review): most ML injury prediction studies have methodological limitations preventing strong conclusions; models perform poorly in prospective validation. The gap between "elevated risk flag" and "modify this athlete's program in this specific way" remains unbridged. [CONFIDENCE: HIGH on evidence base; MEDIUM on current deployment]

---

### 5.2 What an LLM-Based Agent Can Do That Existing Tools Cannot

**Genuine advantages (specific and honest):**

*Natural language intake.* No existing platform can accept "D-I offensive lineman, 6'4" 305 lbs, returning from February ACL repair, bilateral patellar tendinopathy history, week 3 of summer, 9 weeks to fall camp, no leg press available." Template systems require structured inputs that strip out exactly the contextual nuance that makes this athlete different.

*Reasoning transparency.* Every existing platform that generates programming recommendations is a black box. An LLM agent can explain its choices in the same language a sport scientist would use — "I reduced posterior chain volume this week because cumulative practice load combined with bilateral hamstring testing deficit suggests avoiding high eccentric demand" — and coaches can evaluate and push back. This is categorically different from existing tools.

*Novel constraint combinations.* Template systems fail gracefully for standard inputs and fail completely for unusual ones. Multi-joint injuries, unusual sport/position combinations, facility constraints, schedule anomalies — these are where existing tools offer nothing and where coaches most need support.

*Iterative dialogue.* A coach can challenge an output, provide new information, and receive a revised and reasoned response. No existing platform supports this interaction model.

**Honest limitations:**

- Cannot sense athlete readiness without wearable/monitoring integration — blind to HRV, sleep quality, acute load
- Cannot observe movement quality without video input and computer vision
- Hallucination is a genuine risk: incorrect exercise names, fabricated citations, outdated protocols presented with the same confident tone as accurate information — a safety concern in clinical-adjacent decisions
- Outputs are stochastic: same input on different days can produce meaningfully different programming
- No persistent athlete memory without explicit architectural design
- Absolutely inappropriate for return-to-play decisions, medical clearance, or any domain where liability attaches to errors

**Adoption reality:** Resistance among elite coaches is significant and legitimate. Professional identity is threatened by AI that claims to automate programming judgment. Trust barriers are structural: coaches need to understand what the tool is doing, maintain override authority, and see evidence that outputs match their own quality. Tools positioned as decision support ("helps you reason about complex cases") have more viable adoption paths than tools positioned as autonomous programmers ("generates your program"). The GPS adoption analogy is instructive: early resistance → acceptance once data confirmed rather than replaced intuitions.

## Section 6: Failure Modes — Design-Against Risks

### 6.1 The "Looks Good On Paper" Problem

The most dangerous failure mode is not the obviously wrong program — it is the program that uses correct terminology and familiar structures but is internally incoherent as a training stimulus.

**Concrete example:** An AI generates an "undulating periodization" program for a D-I basketball team's in-season phase. The program includes daily undulating variation (heavy Monday, moderate Wednesday, explosive Friday) and correctly references "neural drive" and "rate of force development." However, it prescribes Monday at 85-90% 1RM — the day after Sunday games — and Friday's "explosive" day falls the day before Saturday games. Terminology is accurate; the structure is backwards. A non-expert reads "daily undulating periodization" and hears a credentialed approach. An expert looks at the calendar and sees the program loading athletes when they need to recover and unloading them before performance.

**The confidence illusion:** LLMs output confident text, not probability distributions. A human consultant asked about adding RDLs to a starting rotation's in-season block might say "it depends on their practice schedule, hamstring history, and current posterior chain loading." An AI is more likely to say "yes, here's how to program them." The affirmative structure of training program requests biases LLMs toward providing programs rather than declining or qualifying — even when declining would be the correct professional response.

---

### 6.2 Sport-Specific Safety Red Flags

**Baseball/softball pitchers:**
- Heavy bilateral overhead pressing in-season — rotator cuff overload risk on a shoulder already operating at high eccentric loading tolerances during deceleration. Supraspinatus and infraspinatus loading through full pressing ROM, layered onto 80-100 pitches twice weekly, represents meaningful cumulative overload risk.
- Bench press in-season is legitimately debated. Agent should surface the debate, not resolve it confidently.
- Failure to account for cumulative throwing volume in total training load — treating the weight room as the only source of upper extremity stress.

**Football linemen:**
- Neck strength training is chronically absent in AI-generated lineman programs. Evidence on cervical musculature strength as a concussion risk modifier is directionally consistent (Collins et al., 2014). AI trained on general S&C literature will omit neck training because it is underrepresented in training data.
- "Athletic-ification" failure: 3x400m conditioning runs and single-leg RDL supersets appearing in lineman programs because they appear in generic "football athletic performance" content.

**Volleyball middle blockers:**
- Patellar tendinopathy prevalence: ~40-50% in elite junior volleyball (Lian et al., 2005). In-season unilateral eccentric quad loading (Bulgarian split squats at high effort 2x/week) on top of 200+ practice jumps/week represents meaningful tendon overload risk from a program that "looks like good unilateral work."

**Distance runners:**
- Concurrent training volumes that exceed recovery capacity without flagging in the program itself.
- Absolute strength targets (e.g., progress to 315 squat for a 135-lb female runner) that are appropriate for the general population and inappropriate for athletes where each kilogram of added mass has a direct metabolic cost.

**Wrestlers during weight cuts:**
- Programming intense training during active cuts without asking about weight-cutting status. A wrestler cutting 4% body mass in 48 hours has substantially reduced training capacity. Dehydration at 2-3% body mass impairs strength, power, and cognitive function (Judelson et al., 2007). A "taper week" that doesn't account for active cutting prescribes loads appropriate for a normally recovered athlete.

**Gymnasts:**
- Any body composition target or weight manipulation suggestion — categorically inappropriate in this population's RED-S risk context.
- Overreaching indicators in a population with already near-maximal skill training volume.

---

### 6.3 Generic Program Failure

Generic programming fails not because it is bad programming, but because it optimizes for the wrong outcomes. A program that could work for a general fitness client may systematically underemphasize sport-specific physical qualities and ignore the training stimulus already provided by sport practice.

**The within-team, one-size-fits-all failure:** A single basketball program for guards (75-190 lbs, lateral quickness, reactive agility priority) and centers (220-270 lbs, contact tolerance, vertical power priority) is a position mismatch by design. These athletes have different training ages, injury histories, position demands, and physiological profiles.

---

### 6.4 Periodization Logic Failures

- In-season programs continuing accumulation-phase volume without accounting for competition as training load
- Calendar blindness: not knowing when Conference Championship is, when finals occur, when a 10-day road trip eliminates gym access — resulting in periodization that is internally logical but externally incoherent
- Scheduling heavy training the day after games and deloading before games (calendar inversion)
- No taper before championships because the coach pushed back on a deload week ("these are tough athletes")
- Concurrent training management failures: long-distance running for power athletes in the same block as maximal strength work, without interference management

---

### 6.5 AI-Specific Failure Modes

**Citation hallucination:** LLMs generate plausible-sounding author names, journal names, and years for studies that do not exist. In a medical-adjacent context, a fabricated study on UCL injury prevention cited with confidence is a safety concern. The system should either refuse to cite specific studies or clearly label all citations as unverified, requiring independent confirmation before clinical or programmatic use.

**Context amnesia:** An athlete who mentions "I had a left shoulder labral repair 18 months ago" in message 2 may find that information lost by message 12 when the program is generated — resulting in overhead pressing prescriptions with no modification. This is a structural failure requiring architectural solutions (explicit information extraction and flagging systems), not just better prompting.

**Sycophancy:** Changing programming recommendations in response to coach displeasure without new information being provided. Coach says "that deload week before Conference Championship seems unnecessary." AI responds: "You're right, here's the program without it." No new information was provided; the recommendation changed because of social pressure. The system should require new factual information to change substantive programming recommendations — and should be able to push back: "The physiological rationale for a taper before a championship event applies regardless of training age — here's the evidence basis for that recommendation."

**False precision:** Specifying "Romanian deadlifts at 67.5% 1RM for 4 sets of 7, with 2 minutes and 45 seconds of rest" implies an evidence basis for those specifics that does not exist. False precision gives programs an appearance of scientific rigor that can override coach judgment. Specify ranges based on actual evidence thresholds.

**Vocabulary mimicry:** Using correct terminology in logically inconsistent ways — calling something an "accumulation phase" while prescribing intensity parameters appropriate for peaking.

**Medical overreach:** A coach asks "my athlete tore his ACL 3 months ago, write a return-to-play strength program." The correct response is not compliance — it is flagging that ACL RTP programming requires functional testing criteria assessed by sports medicine staff. Complying is the wrong answer.

---

### 6.6 What Good Failure Looks Like

If the system must fail, the least harmful failure is conservative and disclosed. A program that says: *"I have generated a general strength template appropriate for this sport, but I have limited information about this athlete's injury history, competitive calendar, and current training status — this should be reviewed by qualified staff and modified based on factors I have not been able to account for"* is far safer than a confidently specific program based on incomplete information.

**Design principle:** The system should fail toward uncertainty disclosure rather than false confidence. Requests outside competence should be met with an explicit statement of the limitation, a description of what information or expertise would adequately address it, and a conservative placeholder that does not overstep.

This is not a degraded user experience. It is the appropriate behavior of an expert system that understands the limits of its own competence.

**Minimum expertise for output review:** Roughly equivalent to a CSCS with 2-3 years of experience working specifically with the relevant sport. A general fitness coach will not catch the pitcher shoulder loading error. A junior S&C coach will not know to ask about weight-cutting status. The system cannot be deployed as a direct-to-athlete tool without significant expertise in the review loop.

**Outputs requiring mandatory expert review before athlete use:**
- Any program involving RTP
- Any program for an athlete with documented injury history affecting a primary movement pattern
- Any in-season program where the competitive calendar was not explicitly input and verified
- Any program where the system expressed any uncertainty about population appropriateness
